The homework problem I found most valuable is Problem 1 from Homework 5, which focuses on the cylindrical pin fin (assuming an adiabatic tip) and calculating fin effectiveness, fin efficiency, thermal resistance, the overall efficiency of a fin array, and the thermal resistance of a fin array. The problem also examines how much heat is dissipated through the unfinned surface compared to the finned surface, and finally analyzes a heat sink consisting of the fin array. This assignment was particularly valuable because it brought together many of the key lessons from the class, including heat transfer mechanisms, thermal resistance networks, and energy balances involving conduction, convection, and radiation.

This topic is also interesting to me because I have seen a real-world application of heat sinks. This past summer, I worked on a project where heat sinks were used to dissipate heat generated by a camera flying in the stratosphere. Even though the stratosphere itself is very cold, the system could get extremely hot when it was near the ground, especially in the Mojave Desert in California, so a heat sink was critical for the system to operate properly. While I previously understood the basic idea that heat transfers from hot to cold, this homework assignment helped me develop a deeper understanding of how to analyze and design thermal systems. From this problem I gained analytical tools such as calculating fin efficiency and effectiveness, estimating temperature distributions along fins, building thermal resistance networks, and evaluating the overall performance of heat sink arrays. These methods are directly applicable to aerospace systems such as cooling avionics and onboard electronics, managing heat in satellite instruments and cameras, thermal control of batteries and power electronics, and designing heat sinks for sensors or propulsion-related components. As a mechanical engineer interested in aerospace design and manufacturing, understanding how to model and predict heat dissipation like this is critical for ensuring systems operate safely and reliably.
